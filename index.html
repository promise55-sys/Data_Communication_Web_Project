Core Concepts

What I've Learnt in Data Communication

Data communication is the discipline of transferring digital information reliably between devices across a medium. This course gave me a structured understanding of signals, encoding, protocols, and the architecture of the networks that link our world together.

📡

Transmission Media

Data travels through guided media — twisted pair, coaxial cable, and fibre optics — and unguided media such as radio waves and microwaves. Each medium has distinct bandwidth, attenuation, and noise characteristics that determine its best application environment.

🔁

Network Protocols & the OSI Model

Protocols are the agreed rules governing communication. The 7-layer OSI model separates communication into independent layers — physical, data link, network, transport, session, presentation, and application — each with a specific, well-defined responsibility.

📶

Modulation & Signal Encoding

Before data travels, it must be encoded onto a carrier signal. I studied AM, FM, and PM modulation for analogue systems, and digital encoding schemes — NRZ, Manchester, QPSK — that ensure bits are represented reliably over a physical medium.

🛡

Error Detection & Correction

Noise and interference corrupt signals in transit. Parity bits, CRC, and checksums detect errors. Forward Error Correction (FEC) codes such as Hamming and LDPC allow the receiver to reconstruct correct data without requesting retransmission from the sender.

🔀

Multiplexing Techniques

FDM, TDM, and CDMA allow multiple signals to share one physical channel simultaneously. This efficient sharing of bandwidth is foundational to cellular networks, broadband internet, and satellite communication systems around the world.

🌐

Network Topologies

Bus, star, ring, mesh, and hybrid topologies each offer different trade-offs in fault tolerance, scalability, and cost. I can now evaluate a real environment and recommend the most appropriate topology for its purpose and operating constraints.

🔐

Data Link Layer & MAC

The data link layer frames raw bits into structured packets and manages access to a shared medium through CSMA/CD (Ethernet) and CSMA/CA (Wi-Fi). MAC addresses uniquely identify devices at this layer to ensure frames reach the correct destination.

📈

Bandwidth & Shannon's Theorem

Bandwidth is the range of frequencies a channel can carry; data rate is the bits per second transmitted. Shannon's theorem defines the theoretical maximum data rate of a noisy channel — a foundational limit that governs every communication system ever designed.

Historical Foundation

The Electric Telegraph — Where It All Began

Invented around 1837 by Samuel Morse and Alfred Vail, the electric telegraph was the world's first long-distance electrical data communication system. An operator pressed a Morse key to open and close an electrical circuit, sending coded electrical pulses along a copper wire strung between wooden poles. At the receiving end those pulses energised an electromagnet that clicked a metal arm — producing sounds decoded as letters and words. This single principle — encoding information as electrical signals and transmitting them over a medium to a decoder — is identical to how every modern network works today.

Figure 1 — Electric Telegraph System (c.1837). The operator presses the Morse key, closing the circuit; current flows from the battery along the copper wire (supported on insulated poles) to the electromagnet at the receiver, which pulls a sounder arm producing audible clicks decoded as dots and dashes. The earth ground completes the return path.

⚡

How the Circuit Works

Pressing the Morse key closes the electrical circuit. Current flows from the battery, along the copper wire, through the electromagnet coil at the receiver, and returns via the earth ground. The electromagnet pulls the sounder arm — a short click is a dot; a long click is a dash.

🖰

Morse Code as Digital Encoding

Morse code maps every letter and digit to a unique dot-dash sequence — a binary-like digital encoding system representing information as two distinct signal states long before electronic computers existed. A = · —   S = · · ·   O = — — —   SOS = · · · — — — · · ·

📋

Why Insulators Matter

Ceramic insulators mounted the live copper wire onto wooden poles without letting current leak into the pole or ground prematurely. This is an early practical application of electrical isolation — the same principle used in modern PCB design and fibre optic cable jacketing.

🔗

Connection to Modern Networks

The telegraph established the fundamental model of all data communication: source → encoding → medium → receiver → decoding. Replace the Morse key with a router, the copper wire with fibre optic cable, and the sounder with a server — and you have the internet.

Last Assignment Summary

Satellite Data Communication

My term paper — Satellite Data Communication: Orbital Systems, Signal Transmission, Zooming Technology, and Future Trajectories — provided a comprehensive examination of how data moves across space. The key findings are summarised below. Click the button to open the full PDF assignment in a new tab.

GEO Orbit
35,786 km

Geostationary satellites appear stationary from Earth, allowing fixed dish antennas. They introduce ~480ms round-trip latency — ideal for broadcast TV, weather satellites, and VSAT enterprise services but unsuitable for real-time interactive applications.

LEO Orbit
200–2,000 km

Low Earth orbit constellations like Starlink (4,000+ satellites by 2023) achieve 20–40ms latency comparable to fibre broadband. Large satellite arrays provide continuous global coverage for broadband internet and IoT applications.

MEO Orbit
~150ms RTT

GPS and O3b/SES operate in MEO, balancing coverage with latency. Approximately 31 GPS satellites at 20,200km provide global precision navigation. O3b serves maritime and aeronautical broadband users at 150ms round-trip delay.

Satellite Zooming
100s beams

Digital beamforming generates hundreds of simultaneous independent beams from a single antenna aperture. Beam hopping reallocates capacity across geographic cells in real time, matching demand dynamically and maximising spectral efficiency.

Key Challenges
⚠

Rain fade, orbital debris and the Kessler syndrome risk, spectrum congestion between NGSO constellations, the 2022 Viasat cyberattack, and affordability barriers in developing nations remain critical and partially unresolved challenges.

Future: 5G & AI
5G NTN

3GPP Release 17 formally integrates satellites into 5G Non-Terrestrial Networks. AI and machine learning now drive beam scheduling, fault detection, and traffic prediction — shifting satellite operations from reactive to fully proactive management.
